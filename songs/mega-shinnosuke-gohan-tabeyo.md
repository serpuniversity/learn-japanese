## Word-by-Word Breakdown (selected lines)

<details>
<summary>ごはんを食べると元気になれるヨ</summary>

- ごはん (gohan) = cooked rice / a meal  
- を (o) = object marker  
- 食べる (taberu) = to eat  
- と (to) = “when/if” (natural consequence)  
- 元気 (genki) = energy / feeling well  
- に (ni) = “into / to become”  
- なれる (nareru) = can become (potential form)  
- ヨ (yo) = sentence-ending emphasis (“you know!”)

→ “When you eat, you can feel better.”
</details>

<details>
<summary>どんな天才も 腹はへるんだよ</summary>

- どんな (donna) = whatever kind of  
- 天才 (tensai) = genius  
- も (mo) = even / also  
- 腹 (hara) = stomach / belly  
- は (wa) = topic marker  
- へる (heru) = to get hungry (lit. “to decrease”)  
- んだよ (n da yo) = explanatory + emphasis

→ “Even a genius gets hungry.”
</details>

## Grammar Patterns

1. **〜たら (tara)**: “if/when” (conditional)  
   - おなかがすいたら = “If/when you’re hungry…”

2. **V(ます)-に行こう (ni ikou)**: “Let’s go do (verb)”  
   - 食べにいこう = “Let’s go eat.”

3. **〜たり〜たりする (tari…tari suru)**: listing examples (“do things like…”)  
   - 病んだりしちゃう = “end up feeling worn out (and stuff).”

4. **〜ずに (zu ni)**: “without doing”  
   - 待たずに = “without waiting.”

5. **〜と (to)**: natural consequence (“when you do X, Y happens”)  
   - 食べると元気になれる = “When you eat, you can feel better.”

## Notes on Tone

- **ヨ (yo)** adds a casual, friendly emphasis (like “you know” / “I’m telling you”).
- The chorus is deliberately simple and encouraging: food as an easy reset button for rough days.
